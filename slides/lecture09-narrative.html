Framing your analysis

How you structure

We “answer” questions like:

  • What is the decision problem?
  • Who are the decision-makers?
  • What are the goals?
  • What are the possible actions?
  • What are the relevant processes?
  • What are the uncertainties?

How you analyze

We “answer” questions like:

  • What is feasible to analyze?
  • Do I optimize or not?
  • What model(s) do I use?
  • How do I sample uncertainties?
  • How do I measure objectives?

Discussion questions

  • How are the tasks of structuring and analyzing related?
  • Which task did the course provide more guidance on?
  • What are some differences between a DA project, paper, and presentation?

Framing as narrative

General guidance

  • Who is the audience?
  • Assert how decisions are made
  • Emphasize decision relevance
  • Emphasize structure
  • Justify technical details and describe them as simply as possible

Project-level guidance

  • Audience: funders, clients, stakeholders
  • Emphasis: improving a real-world decision
  • Technical: be as good as the best and improve a few key things

Paper-level guidance

  • Audience: scientists, funders, agencies
  • Emphasis: decision insights or better method to analyze decisions
  • Technical: be as good as the best and improve one or two things

Abstract-level guidance

“The abstract is of utmost importance, for it is read by 10 to 500 times more people than hear or read the entire article. It should not be a mere recital of the subjects covered, replete with such expressions as ‘is discussed’ and ‘is described.’ It should be a condensation and concentration of the essential qualities of the paper.”

— Kenneth Landes. A Scrutiny of the Abstract. Bulletin of the American Association of Petroleum Geologists, Vol. 35, No. 7, July 1951, p. 1660.

Also see Nature summary guidance

Presentation-level guidance

  • Audience: varies, often your largest and most diverse
  • Emphasis: same as paper, but even more focused
  • Technical: cater to audience and be mindful of time

Deconstructing a 15-minute presentation

  • Audience: Annual Conference of the Society for Decision Making Under Deep Uncertainty
  • Emphasis: decision insight
  • Technical: None!
    • Reference rigorous methods in speech, prepare extra slides, know your stuff for questions

Let’s deconstruct this example with the following in mind:

  • What decision insight does it emphasize?
  • How and when does it establish the decision problem?
  • How much “technical” detail does it cover?
  • What is the figure size to slide ratio?
  • What is the figure to text ratio?
  • What style features does it employ for emphasis?
  • How many times is each point (motivation, technical, result) repeated in one way or another?
